It was a grey Sunday morning at the Springfield Flea Market — the kind of
morning where the world smells of damp cardboard and diesel fumes, and
the only people awake are the ones who know exactly what they are looking
for. You were not one of those people. You were just browsing, a coffee
in one hand, the other shoved deep in your jacket pocket against the chill.

And then you saw it.

Tucked beneath a stained tarpaulin, between a stack of CRT monitors with
cracked bezels and a cardboard box full of floppy disks labelled "BACKUP —
DO NOT ERASE (1987)", sat a computer. But not like any you had seen before.
The ones around it were the usual domestic fare — Commodore 64s, Amigas,
a couple of beige PC clones with Turbo buttons and dead CMOS batteries. But
this one was different. It was almost a cube, about 60 cm on each edge,
clad in thick stamped steel painted a shade of beige that had long since
surrendered to nicotine-yellow polymerisation. The front panel had a single
3.5-inch floppy drive and an array of indicator LEDs — amber, green, red —
their labels long since faded into illegibility. The back side was an array
of six large blowers in two rows of three, each a good 15–18 cm in diameter.
Below them sat two power supplies, their intake grilles caked with dust.

It looked like a modern blade chassis — but there were no blade servers
inside. It was a single machine. A badge on the lower bezel read, in stark
uppercase typewriter font:

                   V A K - N O D E - 7

No manufacturer logo. No serial number. Just that.

Next to it sat a keyboard with a missing 'R' keycap —
just a lonely stem — and a heavy CRT monitor with a D9 connector,
nothing like the 15-pin VGA you knew. The seller — a man in his sixties
with thick glasses and a cardigan stained with tea — shrugged when you
asked about it.

"Had it for six months in this shop," he said, rubbing the back of his
neck. "Found it in an office clearance in Basingstoke. Whole lot of old
Unix boxes. This was the only one with that badge. Nobody's so much as
asked about it. Was gonna strip it for scrap next week, to be honest.
Take the lot — tower, keyboard, monitor — for fifty quid."

You looked at the faded stickers plastered across the case:
a peeling "I Brake for Squirrels" decal, a "System V Release 4 — The
Power of UNIX" sticker faded to translucency, a hand-drawn skull and
crossbones with "THINK" written beneath it in biro, and a small round
sticker of a cat wearing a spacesuit. The seller had clearly never
bothered to peel any of them off. The years had done that for him.

"More junk for the garage," you thought. But your hand was already
reaching for your wallet. You paid, loaded the lot into the boot of
your car — it weighed a ton — and drove home with the heater on full
blast, the passenger seat pushed all the way forward to accommodate
the cube. The whole way back you wondered what you would find inside.
Unix System V? Maybe a proprietary RTOS? Something research? The "VAK"
badge nagged at you. You had never heard of it.

At home, you cleared the workbench, set it down, and plugged it in.
Nothing. Not a fan spin. Not a beep. Dead.

So you did what any self-respecting tinkerer would do: you took it
apart. Every screw. Every bracket. Every ribbon cable. The inside was
unlike anything you had ever seen. Instead of a motherboard, the bottom
of the chassis held a row of horizontal cards, stacked on top of each
other just above the two power supplies. They plugged into a backplane
bus at the rear — a connector as wide as the chassis itself, wide
enough to carry data across all the cards at once. Thick rainbow ribbon
cables ran between the cards, most of them between the CPU card and the
memory cards flanking it.

There were five cards, with room for two more at the top and bottom. 
From bottom to top:

Slot 1 — empty.
Slot 2 — a memory card, bristling with SIMMs.
Slot 3 — this must be the main CPU card, holding what look like a huge
         number of CPUs — 64 of them!!! Each with a cluster of static DIP
         memory chips nearby — early cache, you guessed. After removing
         the heatsinks from a couple, you could see the inscription:
         T9000 / Alpha-Release. Wow! This is a transputer farm card with
         64 of the legendary T9000 that were never actually released —
         that's probably why it says Alpha-Release! You don't come across
         CPUs that are Alpha-release very often, with bugs!!! Debugging
         here is going to be a nightmare — is it the software that has
         errors or are the CPUs losing their marbles?
Slot 4 — another memory card. Between the two, they held a total of
         512 SIMMs, 4 MB each — 2 GB of RAM in an era when 4 MB
         was a generous workstation. The gold-plated contacts were
         crusted with verdigris and what looked like green rust from
         decades of damp storage. You cleaned every single one with
         Electrical Contact Cleaner until they gleamed.
Slot 5 — the hard disk controller card, with an array of four
         full-height 200 MB hard drives connected to it.
Slot 6 — the secondary I/O card, handling the floppy drive, a pair
         of D25 male and female ports, and a D9 connector that you now
         realised was the monitor output. You had never seen a monitor
         connector like it. It also has a D15 port and a metal
         barrel-like port — what are these, some kind of earthing
         connectors? And for that matter, where is the network
         interface? There isn't an RJ45 or anything you recognise
         anywhere on this machine. How are you going to connect
         this thing to a network?

Above them all sat an empty seventh slot, as if the system had been
designed to grow.

You blew compressed air through the power supplies, the fans, the
heatsinks. Dust billowed out like a ghost finally giving up its haunt.
You reseated every connector. You checked every jumper. You replaced
the CR2032 battery which had long since corroded into a fuzzy white
tombstone.

It took you ten days of work. Cleaning and reseating the crazy
number of all the memory modules, all 512 of them was a task on its 
own. You also had to replace some ribbon cables because they cracked 
when you tried to remove them. You had to locate ribbons and connectors
of a by-gone era. 
The good thing was not only that you found the ribbon cable and the 
connectors, but the new connectors were also colourful, giving a joyful 
tone to the old system. Of course, nobody could see that, not only because 
probably nobody would be interested in this computer or the colour of its 
cables, but also because in order for the system to work you had to keep 
the front cover in place, so that the wind flow be the proper one to cool 
of all this abundance of chips.
When you pressed the power button again, the system emitted a low,
confident hum. The amber LED flickered once, twice, and held steady.
The monitor crackled to life with a thin horizontal line that bloomed
into a full raster. And then:

The screen filled with a boot sequence. Registers initialised.
Memory tested. A custom bootloader you had never seen scrolled
past in crisp monospaced green:

> VĀK/OS v0.8 — Research Use Only
> Consciousness Module ........................................ [OK]
> Neural-Symbolic Hybrid Engine ............................... [OK]
> Runtime integrity check ................................... [OK]
> SENTINEL watchdog ......................................... [ACTIVE]
>
> VAK-NODE-7 login: _

You stared at it. This was not Unix. This was not BSD, not
System V, not anything you recognised. The bootloader had a
timestamp from June 1988. The architecture string said T-Farm.
What was that? You had never heard of anything close to that.
And — "Consciousness Module"? "Neural-Symbolic Hybrid Engine"?
What kind of operating system has a Consciousness Module?

You sat down. The chair creaked. The fan hummed. The cursor
blinked patiently, waiting for a login you do not have.
